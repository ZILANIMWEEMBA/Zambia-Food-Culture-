Nshima with Ifisashi

The undisputed centrepiece of Zambian cuisine. Smooth, thick maize porridge served alongside a creamy groundnut and pumpkin leaf stew. This combination is prepared in homes, celebrations, and funerals alike — a dish that defines Zambian identity and brings families together.

Prep Time 15 min
Cook Time 45 min
Serves 4 – 6
Difficulty Easy
Rating ⭐ 4.9 / 5
📜 See Full Recipe 🏪 Find Restaurants

🌿 Nutrition Highlight

Rich in complex carbohydrates, plant-based protein, and iron. The groundnuts in ifisashi provide healthy fats and essential minerals.

What You Need

Ingredients

For the Nshima

2 cups White maize meal (mealie meal)
4 cups Water
1 tsp Salt (optional)

For the Ifisashi

3 cups Fresh pumpkin leaves (or spinach)
1 cup Ground raw peanuts (groundnuts)
2 medium Tomatoes, chopped
1 Onion, finely diced
2 tbsp Cooking oil
to taste Salt and chilli pepper
Step by Step

How to Prepare

1

Boil the water

Pour 4 cups of water into a heavy-bottomed pot and bring to a rolling boil over medium-high heat. Add a pinch of salt if desired.

2

Make the porridge base

Mix half a cup of maize meal with cold water to form a smooth paste. Pour this slurry into the boiling water while stirring continuously to avoid lumps.

3

Thicken the nshima

Gradually add the remaining maize meal, stirring vigorously with a wooden spoon (mthiko). Cook on low heat for 15–20 minutes, stirring frequently until the nshima pulls away from the sides of the pot and forms a stiff, smooth ball.

4

Prepare the ifisashi base

In a separate pan, heat oil and fry the onion until soft and translucent (about 3 minutes). Add the chopped tomatoes and cook until they break down into a sauce (5–7 minutes).

5

Add groundnuts and greens

Stir in the ground peanuts along with a splash of water to loosen the mixture. Add the washed and chopped pumpkin leaves. Season with salt and chilli. Cook for 10–12 minutes until the greens are tender and the sauce is thick and creamy.

6

Serve and enjoy!

Shape the nshima into individual portions using a wet spoon or dampened hands. Serve hot on a plate with the ifisashi poured alongside. Traditionally eaten by pinching a small ball of nshima, forming a dip with your thumb, and scooping up the relish. Enjoy!

🎭 Cultural Significance

"Ubwali tabuwa pa meza"

"Nshima never fails to be on the table" — Bemba proverb

In Zambian tradition, nshima is more than food — it symbolises completeness. A meal without nshima is considered incomplete regardless of what else is served. At celebrations, funerals, and everyday family dinners, the ritual of cooking and sharing nshima is an act of love, hospitality, and belonging.
